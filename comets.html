Comets

About Mars

Comets are icy bodies of frozen gases, rocks and dust left over from the formation of the solar system about 4.6 billion years ago. They orbit the sun in highly elliptical orbits that can take hundreds of thousands of years to complete. As a comet approaches the sun, it heats up very quickly causing solid ice to turn directly into gas via a process called sublimation, according to the Lunar and Planetary Institute(opens in new tab). The gas contains water vapor, carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide and other trace substances, and is eventually swept into the distinctive comet tail. Scientists sometimes call comets dirty snowballs or snowy dirtballs, depending on whether they contain more ice material or rocky debris according to NASA

Comet 103P/Hartley (Hartley 2

Hartley 2 was discovered by Malcolm Hartley on Mar. 15, 1986 using the Schmidt Telescope at the Siding Spring Observatory in Australia.

109P/Swift-Tuttle

Comet Swift-Tuttle was discovered in 1862 independently by both Lewis Swift and Horace Tuttle.

19P/Borrelly

Resembling a chicken leg, the nucleus of comet 19P/Borrelly is small and measures approximately 2.98 miles (4.8 kilometers) in diameter.

1P/Halley

Comet Halley is perhaps the most famous comet.

21P/Giacobini-Zinner

It takes comet 21P/Giacobini-Zinner 6.59 years to orbit the sun once.

21/Borisov

Comet 2I/Borisov is the first confirmed interstellar comet. It was discovered by Crimean amateur astronomer Gennady Borisov on Aug. 30, 2019, and quickly became a global phenomenon.
